What We Do

How We
Build Fear

A long answer to a short question.

We Don't Build Jump Scares. We Build Rooms.

Take the screen away and horror changes shape. On a monitor, fear is something that happens to a character you're watching. In a headset, there's no character — there's just you, standing in a hallway that's a little too quiet, holding a controller a little too tight. That difference is the entire reason Splash Studios exists.

We don't chase loud stings or things flying at the camera. Those tricks rely on a frame around the screen — something VR doesn't have. Instead we design around presence: the simple, physical discomfort of occupying a space that might not be empty. A flickering light reads differently when you can't look away from it without turning your actual head. A sound behind you means something when "behind you" is a real direction in a real room.

Mechanics come second. Scares come last. Presence comes first, every time.

The Process

Nothing we make starts as a pitch document or a level map. It starts as one small loop — a hallway, a sound, a thing that might be listening — built rough enough to walk around in within a day or two. From there, the rule is simple: if it isn't unsettling standing up, headset on, it doesn't matter how it reads on a monitor.

That means most of our actual development time happens in-headset, not at a desk. A lot of what feels tense on a flat screen feels silly once you're standing in it, and a lot of what feels too quiet on a monitor turns out to be unbearable in VR. We don't trust our assumptions about what's scary — we trust what we watch happen to a real person wearing the headset: where their hands go, where they actually look, when they back into a corner without realizing it.

Sound design isn't a layer we add at the end — it's built alongside the space itself, because in headset, sound is frequently doing more work than anything you can see. And we build in small, shippable pieces rather than scoping an enormous world on day one. A short experience that's tight and considered beats a long one padded out to look bigger than it is.

What We Believe

01

Presence Over Polish

A rough room that feels real beats a beautiful one that feels like a screen.

02

Sound Is the Monster

A huge share of the horror budget goes to your ears before it ever goes to your eyes.

03

Small Spaces, Big Dread

A corridor you can reach out and touch is scarier than a vista you can't.

04

Built Standing Up

If we can't test it on our feet, headset on, lights off, we don't ship it.

05

Players Write the Story

We build the conditions for fear. The player supplies the panic.

06

Ship Small, Ship Often

A short game that's tight beats a long one that's padded.

Why "Splash"

The name has nothing to do with the genre, and that's sort of the point. We didn't want to box ourselves into a name that only makes sense if we only ever make horror games — we just happen to be good at horror right now. "Splash" is short, blunt, and easy to remember, which is really all a studio name needs to do. Everything else is up to what we build next.
